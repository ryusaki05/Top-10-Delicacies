Bulalo

  • 2-3 lbs beef shanks (bone-in, marrow-rich)
  • 10 cups water
  • 1 large onion,quartered
  • 2 cloves garlic, crushed
  • 1/2 cup chopped green onions
  • 1/4 cup chopped cilantro
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • 1/2 cup chopped cabbage (optional)
  • 1/2 cup sliced bok choy (optional)
  • 1/2 cup sliced green beans(optional)
  • 1/4 cup patis (fish sauce, optional)

Instruction:

  • In a large pot or Dutch oven, combine the beef shanks, water, onion, and garlic. Bring to a boil over high heat.
  • Reduce heat to low, cover, and simmer fo 3-4 hours, or until the beef is very tender and the bone marrow is easily extracted.
  • Remove the beef shanks from the pot and set aside.
  • Skim off any excess fat from the broth.
  • Add the green onions, cilantro, salt, and pepper to the broth.
  • If using, Add the cabbage, bok choy, and green beans. Simmer for 5 minutes , or until the vegetables are tender-crisp.
  • If using, stir in the patis (fish sauce) for extra flavor
  • Return the beef shanks to the pot.
  • Serve hot in bowls, with rice or noodles.

    Pancit

    • 1 tablespoon vegetable oil
    • 1/2 cup sliced pork or chicken
    • 1/2 cup sliced onion
    • 1/2 cup sliced green bell pepper
    • 1/2 cup sliced cabbage
    • 1/4 cup soy sauce
    • 1 tablespoon oyster sauce
    • 1 teaspoon sugar
    • 1 teaspoon sesame oil
    • 1/4 teaspoon ground black pepper
    • 1/4 chopped garlic chives (optional)
    • 1/4 cup chopped green onions, for garnish
    • Calamansi juice or lemon juice, for serving (optional)

    Instruction:

      Cook the pancit noodles according to package directions. Drain and set aside.
    • Heat the vegetable oil in large skillet or work over medium-high heat. Add the pork or chicken and cook until browned. Add the onion, green pepper, and cabbage and cook until softened, about 5 minutes.
    • In a small bowl, whisk together the soy sauce, oyster sauce, sugar, sesame oil, and black pepper.
    • Pour the sauce mixture into a skillet and bring to a simmer. Add the cooked noodles and stir to coat.
    • Cook for 2-3 minutes, or until the noodles are heated through. Stir in the garlic chives(if using)
    • Garnish with chopped green onions and serve immediately. Add a squeeze of calamansi or lemon juice for extra tang (optional)

    Lechon Kawali

    Lechon Kawali: A Filipino Crispy Pork Delight

    Lechon Kawali, a Filipino dish that translates to “fried roasted pork,” is a culinary masterpiece that embodies the Filipino love for crispy and flavorful pork. It's a dish that captures the essence of Filipino celebrations, often gracing tables during special occasions and feasts.

    The origins of Lechon Kawali can be traced back to the traditional Filipino method of roasting whole pigs, known as “lechon.” However, Lechon Kawali offers a more convenient and accessible way to enjoy the crispy, savory goodness of pork. It involves marinating pork belly in a mixture of soy sauce, vinegar, garlic, and peppercorns, resulting in a flavorful base. The marinated pork is then coated in cornstarch and deep-fried until it achieves a golden brown and incredibly crispy exterior, while the interior remains tender and juicy.

    Lechon Kawali is a testament to the Filipino culinary creativity and the art of balancing flavors and textures. Its combination of crispiness, savory richness, and tender meat makes it a truly satisfying dish. Whether served as a main course or a side dish, Lechon Kawali is a beloved Filipino dish that never fails to impress.

    View Lechon Kawali Recipe

    Lechon Kawali Recipe

    Ingredients

    • 2 lbs pork belly, skin-on
    • 1/2 cup soy sauce
    • 1/4 cup white vinegar
    • 1/4 cup water
    • 4 cloves garlic, minced
    • 1 tablespoon black peppercorns
    • 1 bay leaf
    • 1 teaspoon salt (or to taste)
    • 1/2 cup cornstarch
    • Vegetable oil, for deep frying

    Procedure

    • In a large bowl, combine the pork belly, soy sauce, vinegar, water, garlic, peppercorns, bay leaf, and salt. Mix well to coat the pork.
    • Cover and marinate in the refrigerator for at least 30 minutes, or up to overnight for a more intense flavor.
    • Remove the pork from the marinade and pat dry with paper towels.
    • In a separate bowl, combine the cornstarch and ¼ cup water. Mix until smooth.
    • Coat the pork belly with the cornstarch mixture, making sure to cover all sides.
    • Heat a large pot or deep fryer filled with vegetable oil over medium-high heat.
    • Carefully lower the pork belly into the hot oil. Fry for about 10-15 minutes, or until golden brown and crispy.
    • Remove the pork belly from the oil and drain on paper towels.
    • Slice the lechon kawali into thick pieces and serve hot.

    Adobo

    Adobo: The National Dish of the Philippines

    Adobo, the national dish of the Philippines, is a testament to the country’s culinary heritage. It’s a dish that transcends generations, bringing families and communities together around a table filled with its savory and comforting flavors.

    The origins of adobo can be traced back to the pre-colonial era. It’s believed that Filipinos, long before the arrival of the Spanish, preserved meat by soaking it in vinegar and salt. This technique, known as “adobo,” proved to be an effective way to extend the shelf life of food, especially in a tropical climate. Over time, the dish evolved with the addition of other ingredients, such as soy sauce, garlic, and peppercorns, giving it the distinct flavor profile that we know and love today.

    Adobo is a remarkably versatile dish, with variations found across different regions of the Philippines. Some variations include adobong tuyo (dried fish adobo), adobong pusit (squid adobo), and adobong gata (coconut milk adobo). Despite the variations, the core essence of adobo remains the same – a flavorful combination of savory, sour, and salty notes that captures the essence of Filipino cuisine.

    Adobo is a dish that transcends culinary boundaries. It’s a dish that’s deeply rooted in Filipino culture, symbolizing unity, tradition, and a sense of belonging. Whether enjoyed with rice, pan de sal (Filipino bread rolls), or simply on its own, adobo continues to be a beloved and iconic dish that embodies the spirit of Filipino gastronomy.

    Ingredients

    • 1 lb boneless, skinless chicken thighs or drumsticks, cut into 1-inch pieces
    • 1/2 cup soy sauce
    • 1/4 cup white vinegar
    • 1/4 cup water
    • 4 cloves garlic, minced
    • 1 tablespoon black peppercorns
    • 1 bay leaf
    • 1 teaspoon salt (or to taste)
    • 1 tablespoon olive oil (optional, for browning)

    Procedure

    1. In a large bowl, combine the chicken, soy sauce, vinegar, water, garlic, peppercorns, bay leaf, and salt. Mix well to coat the chicken.
    2. Cover and marinate in the refrigerator for at least 30 minutes, or up to overnight for a more intense flavor.
    3. Heat the olive oil (if using) in a large skillet or Dutch oven over medium heat.
    4. Add the marinated chicken and cook, stirring occasionally, until browned on all sides.
    5. Pour the marinade into the skillet. Bring to a simmer, then reduce heat to low and cover.
    6. Simmer for 30-45 minutes, or until the chicken is cooked through and the sauce has thickened slightly.
    7. Remove from heat and serve hot over rice.

    Arroz Caldo: A Filipino Rice Porridge Comfort Food

    Arroz caldo, also known as “chicken arroz caldo” or “lugaw” (a term for rice porridge in Tagalog), is a beloved Filipino comfort food dish. It’s a warm and savory rice porridge that’s perfect for a chilly day or anytime you’re craving a comforting meal.

    The origins of Arroz Caldo can be traced back to the influence of Chinese cuisine in the Philippines, particularly the rice porridge known as “congee.” Over time, Filipinos adapted this dish by incorporating local ingredients and flavor profiles, resulting in a unique and comforting rice porridge.

    Arroz Caldo is typically made with chicken broth, rice, and shredded chicken. It’s often seasoned with ginger, garlic, and turmeric, giving it a warm and aromatic flavor. The porridge is usually topped with chopped green onions, cilantro, and a drizzle of fish sauce (patis). Sometimes, a raw egg is cracked into the hot porridge, adding a creamy texture and a touch of richness. It’s often served with a squeeze of calamansi or lemon juice for a bright and tangy finish.

    Arroz Caldo is a dish that is cherished for its simplicity and its ability to warm the soul. It’s a dish that brings comfort and nourishes the body, making it a true staple in Filipino cuisine.

    Arroz Caldo Recipe

    Ingredients

    • 1 cup uncooked rice
    • 6 cups chicken broth or water
    • 1/2 cup cooked chicken, shredded
    • 1/2 cup sliced onion
    • 2 cloves garlic, minced
    • 1/2 teaspoon ground ginger
    • 1/4 teaspoon turmeric powder
    • 1/4 teaspoon salt
    • 1/4 teaspoon black pepper
    • 1/4 cup chopped green onions, for garnish
    • 1/4 cup chopped cilantro, for garnish
    • 1 tablespoon fish sauce (patis, optional)
    • 1 egg (optional, for topping)
    • Calamansi juice or lemon juice, for serving (optional)

    Procedure

    1. In a large pot, combine the rice, chicken broth, chicken, onion, garlic, ginger, turmeric, salt, and pepper. Bring to a boil over medium heat.
    2. Reduce heat to low, cover, and simmer for 20-25 minutes, or until the rice is cooked and the broth is absorbed. Stir occasionally to prevent sticking.
    3. Once cooked, stir in the green onions, cilantro, and fish sauce (if using).
    4. To serve, ladle the arroz caldo into bowls. Crack an egg into each bowl (optional) and let it cook in the hot broth.
    5. Serve hot with a squeeze of calamansi or lemon juice (optional).

    Bringhe

    Biringhe: A Filipino Sweet Rice Delight

    Biringhe, also known as “ginataang kanin,” is a Filipino sweet rice dish that showcases the country’s love for coconut milk and sweet treats. It’s a simple yet delicious dessert that’s perfect for any occasion.

    Biringhe’s origins can be traced back to the use of glutinous rice (malagkit) in Filipino cuisine. Glutinous rice, known for its sticky texture, has been a staple ingredient in many Filipino desserts. The addition of coconut milk, a key element in many Filipino dishes, further enhances the flavor and texture of the rice, creating a creamy and subtly sweet dessert.

    Biringhe is made by simmering glutinous rice in a mixture of coconut milk, water, sugar, and a pinch of salt. This simple combination results in a creamy and subtly sweet rice pudding with a delightful texture. It’s often served warm and garnished with toasted shredded coconut for an extra touch of flavor and texture.

    Biringhe is a versatile dessert that can be enjoyed on its own or served with other sweet treats, such as fruit, leche flan, or halo-halo (a Filipino shaved ice dessert). It’s a reminder of the simple yet delicious flavors that make Filipino cuisine so unique and beloved.

    Biringhe Recipe

    Ingredients

    • 1 cup glutinous rice (malagkit)
    • 1 cup coconut milk
    • 1 cup water
    • 1/2 cup sugar
    • 1/4 teaspoon salt
    • 1/2 cup toasted shredded coconut (optional, for garnish)

    Procedure

    1. Rinse the glutinous rice thoroughly until the water runs clear.
    2. In a saucepan, combine the rice, coconut milk, water, sugar, and salt. Stir well to combine.
    3. Bring the mixture to a boil over medium heat, then reduce heat to low, cover, and simmer for 30-40 minutes, or until the rice is cooked and the liquid is absorbed.
    4. Once cooked, fluff the rice with a fork.
    5. Transfer the biringhe to a serving dish. Garnish with toasted shredded coconut (if using) and serve warm.

    Kare-Kare

    Kare-kare: A Filipino Peanut Stew Delight

    Kare-kare, a beloved Filipino dish, is a testament to the country’s culinary creativity and its use of unique ingredients. This rich and savory peanut stew is a symphony of flavors and textures, offering a truly satisfying and comforting experience.

    The origins of Kare-kare can be traced back to the pre-colonial era, when Filipinos used a variety of ingredients, including peanuts, to create hearty and flavorful stews. Over time, the dish evolved into the Kare-kare we know today, incorporating a combination of peanuts, vegetables, and meat, often beef or pork, to create a uniquely satisfying stew.

    Kare-kare is characterized by its creamy and slightly sweet peanut sauce, which provides a rich base for the stew. It typically features a medley of vegetables, including green beans, bok choy, eggplant, and okra. The stew is often served with a side of bagoong alamang (fermented shrimp paste), which adds a salty and pungent kick to the dish, creating a delightful balance of flavors. The combination of savory, sweet, and tangy elements makes Kare-kare a true culinary masterpiece.

    Kare-kare is a dish that reflects the Filipino love for hearty and comforting food. It’s a dish that’s perfect for sharing with loved ones and enjoying on special occasions. Whether enjoyed as a main course or a side dish, Kare-kare is a true culinary gem that embodies the essence of Filipino cuisine.

    Kare-kare Recipe

    Ingredients

    • 1 lb beef shank or oxtail, cut into 1-inch pieces
    • 1 cup peanut butter
    • 6 cups water
    • 1 large onion, quartered
    • 1 cup sliced green beans
    • 1 cup sliced bok choy or Chinese cabbage
    • 1/2 cup sliced eggplant (optional)
    • 1/2 cup sliced string beans (optional)
    • 1/2 cup sliced okra (optional)
    • 1/4 cup shrimp paste (optional)
    • 1/2 cup bagoong alamang (fermented shrimp paste, optional)
    • Salt and pepper to taste

    Procedure

    1. In a large pot or Dutch oven, combine the beef, peanut butter, water, and onion. Bring to a boil over high heat.
    2. Reduce heat to low, cover, and simmer for 1 hour, or until the beef is tender.
    3. Add the green beans, bok choy, eggplant, string beans, and okra (if using). Simmer for 5 minutes, or until the vegetables are tender-crisp.
    4. If using, stir in the shrimp paste and bagoong alamang.
    5. Season with salt and pepper to taste.
    6. Serve hot over rice. Garnish with chopped long green beans, pechay, and cilantro.
